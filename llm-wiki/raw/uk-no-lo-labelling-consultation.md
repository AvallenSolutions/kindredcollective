## Current guidance (voluntary, DHSC, published December 2018)

- **"Alcohol free"**: products at **0.05% ABV or below**, ABV displayed on label.
- **"De-alcoholised"**: products at **0.5% ABV or below** where alcohol has been removed by manufacturing process.
- **"Non-alcoholic"**: not permitted alongside alcoholic drink names (communion wine excepted).
- **"Low alcohol"**: maximum **1.2% ABV**, with ABV indication required.

The guidance replaced expired Food Labelling Regulations 1996 rules. It is voluntary and non-statutory, but the Food Safety Act 1990 prohibition on misleading labelling gives it enforcement teeth.

## Proposed changes (consultation published 28 September 2023, closed 23 November 2023)

1. Raise the "alcohol free" threshold from 0.05% to **0.5% ABV**, aligning with UK licensing law (Licensing Act 2003 s191 excludes ≤0.5% from the definition of alcohol) and international norms.
2. Allow "alcohol free" for any alcohol-substitute drink meeting the threshold, not only de-alcoholised products.
3. Reconsider "de-alcoholised" as a strength descriptor (potentially retaining it only as a production-method indicator).
4. Clarify ABV labelling: front-of-pack display and online point-of-sale visibility.
5. Add CMO low-risk drinking/pregnancy warnings and age-restriction warnings to no/lo labels.
6. Consider moving descriptor rules from voluntary guidance to legislation.

## Rationale and context

- England's 0.05% threshold is among the world's most restrictive: 27 countries use 0.5% or higher (most of the EU at 0.5%; Finland 2.8%); only the Netherlands uses 0.1%. Hitting 0.05% requires expensive de-alcoholisation equipment, disadvantaging smaller producers, especially for draught.
- Public health framing: supporting substitution among the ~10 million English adults (1 in 5) drinking above the 14-unit weekly guideline — that group accounts for ~30% of consumption. No/lo was only ~1.1% of total alcohol volume in 2021; ~2% of pubs offered no/lo draught.
- Consumer-protection counterweight: qualitative research found consumers expect "alcohol free" to mean devoid of alcohol; 85% of an Alcohol Change UK survey preferred it to mean 0%/0.05%; under 6% accepted 0.5%. Risks flagged for religious, pregnancy and alcohol-use-disorder abstainers. The evidence review found drink-driving risk from 0.5% products minimal.
- The 1.2% ABV category ceiling comes from the Soft Drinks Industry Levy Regulations 2018 (reg 9).

## Status at capture (July 2026)

The 2023 consultation was not concluded before the 2024 general election; the succeeding government promised a further consultation in 2025. Current position therefore remains the 2018 guidance: **"alcohol free" = ≤0.05% ABV in the UK**, even though ≤0.5% products fall outside the licensing definition of alcohol and pay no duty.
